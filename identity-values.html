A recent ethical decision

Last year around the 20 week scan of our unborn baby Flora, we learnt that she had a genetic disorder called Trisomy 18, where the odds of surviving pregnancy, birth or infancy were low. Her hands, feet, palate & back were very disabled. Together with my wonderful wife Anna, we discussed our options of continuing with the pregnancy and letting everything happen “naturally” or terminating the pregnancy early in the hospital.

We didn’t want to end our baby's life, who hadn’t yet had a chance to live. However we were also thinking about how much suffering seeing the pregnancy through might cause. Through birth & her short life Flora would probably feel more pain as she developed more senses, and would doubtfully get to experience the joy that one can feel when living on this planet.

How would her birth impact our daughter Mala who would not live a "normal life" for a potential extended period? I'm sure it would enrich her life to have a baby sister to care for, but might it also be traumatizing?

And for us as her parents, having a healthy baby is hard work. I can only imagine what having a severely disabled baby who you know won’t make it past 3 years old is like. I know there are plenty of other families out there who have gone through similar things, my heart is with them all, and over our journey their hearts have been with us as we decided to terminate Flora with love.

Looking back, we still think that we made the right decision. We did a lot of things to make peace with the situation beforehand, in the moment and afterwards. We still talk about her, and Mala talks in very matter of fact terms: “because Flora died”, “She’s in the stars”, “she didn’t come home”, "I want my Flora". Her raw unfiltered expression is actually quite nice because she doesn't hide behind a fear of causing more pain, which helps to allow us to also experience our feelings fully. Of course we still wish that she was with us and healthy, and sometimes wonder what it would have been like if we saw the pregnancy through.

How culture has influenced my values & Identity

I guess there are a few different cultures and communities that I belong to or have belonged to over the years which have influenced me:

Yoga culture has influenced me a lot to take care of my body, do breathwork, meditate, and practice gratefulness. I think it has helped my growth mindset evolve, a phrase that’s really stuck with me over the years is “yoga practice, not yoga perfect”. It’s also helped me to see how much more we have in common than we do differences.

Skydiving culture, although I haven’t skydived in a decade, going to skydiving school had a big impact on me. At a very impressionable age I was surrounded by people of all different ages, ethnicities & countries, who all had vastly different interests. This was where I broadened my horizons, opening my mind to different ways of life or seeing things, to adventure, and started to embrace being me.

Family

- I come from a loving family who have supported me in whatever I choose to do, and given me a place to return to whenever I’ve needed to, and although I’ve taken them for granted in the past, never again. I love them all and it’s important to me to be there for them.

Podcasts

- Sometimes the people “around you” don't even know your name, face or voice. I listen to Tim Ferris, Lex Fridman, Duncan Trussell, Andrew Huberman & Mr Bill, amongst many others.

I think that listening to these long form podcasts has helped me to appreciate different perspectives.That sometimes there’s no right answer, but valid arguments in multiple directions. Still they strive towards a better understanding of the world, ways towards mastery, having human conversations and exploring the infinite depth that living this life can be.

The local community

- I live in a lovely community where people are friendly. We have a Saturday morning family ritual of heading down to the farmers market where we buy local groceries, baked goods and coffees! We often see our friends there and catch up while our kids play on the playground, and if our friends aren’t there we make new ones. Such a nice way to connect to the local community and have some good vibes.

Identify your strengths and how they will support you during your learning journey.

Work ethic

- I was raised to have a strong work ethic by my Dad who instilled in me an attitude of looking or asking for something else to do at work. Building my own massage business & having a family have strengthened my work ethic too!

I think my work ethic will help me to continuously put in the mahi!

Communication, connecting, listening, expressing my opinion

- A few things have contributed to my communication skills over the years, from previously being quite shy. Streetside fundraising for charities, becoming a yoga teacher, & I think the biggest contributor has been massaging people. In 5 years I have massaged over 800 people, and over 5000 massages. So I have met people from all walks of life, and learnt how to interact with them, how to make them comfortable, find out who they are, how to talk to them about making changes to improve their wellbeing. And listening to them, firstly to find out what they have come in for a massage for, and then how to help them with that.

I think communication is going to be invaluable in communicating with everyone, to learn from everybody, to problem solve with each other, bounce ideas off each other, communicate about projects effectively & resolve any issues.

Commitment

- Not always been a strength of mine. In the first half of adulthood, I dropped out of courses, apprenticeships, relationships & friendships for better and for worse. But For at least the last 7 years I’ve been in a committed relationship, finished every course I started, committed to learning German daily, to my business & the friends & family I still have.

I’ve learnt through these commitments that it’s not always easy, there’s some difficult stuff that comes with committing, but the long term benefits of committing are so rewarding.

I think commitment is going to pay off every time I need to commit code to github! Jokes. Commitment is obviously imperative to learning, and I’m sure there are going to be plenty of situations where committing to working, my team, our projects, and more will be needed.

Adaptability

- I have worked so many jobs over my working life, and while I may not have become an expert at any, I learnt from them all. In each instance I learnt enough to be good at my job. This gives me so much confidence in my ability to learn whatever is needed, and to adapt to different situations.

Things never go exactly as planned, so being adaptable to situations is going to be helpful, and knowing that I can learn whatever I need to now and into the future.

Creative problem solving

- This strength was suggested by Anna (my wife). Whenever a problem strikes, my brain starts looking for solutions. Sometimes we don’t have the resources to solve the problem, or we don’t want to just create rubbish. A recent example: as I was about to throw away broken scissors, I realized they don’t work because the blades are not tight together anymore, so I drilled out the pin, to replace with a nut & bolt which worked perfectly until the nut unwound itself. So now they are sitting, waiting for me to buy a lock nut which should stay on there.

This creative problem solving undoubtedly is helpful in software development, while also remembering that sometimes simple solutions work better than “overly creative”.

Overthinking things

- I may have a tendency to overthink things, and worry that I am not expressing myself correctly, or that I’m making the wrong decision. This could definitely affect my productivity if it happens too much, as it can cause me to question myself, or to over prepare (like this blog post?). I am finding the time boxing and learning competencies helpful for keeping this in check at the moment.

Being used to working by myself

- In recent years of my career I have not been involved in too much collaboration work. Mostly I get my work done by myself, and then massage people. So getting used to working in a team where we all depend on each other will be a learning experience for me.

Admitting mistakes

- Another suggestion from Anna. I don’t like to be wrong. I think I’m getting better at admitting mistakes, but I would say that. Sometimes I’m not able to see my own errors directly in the moment which could cause tension or prevent me from learning something valuable.

But where I think I make up for this is that I really try to own up to those errors upon reflection, apologize if I stepped on any toes, and work on finding a resolution.

Communicating frustrations

- I’m still working on this. It’s not comfortable to address frustrations, especially if it will offend someone. This can be cultural too, right? If the rules of expressing issues are not agreed upon as a group, then people might be more likely to be offended, and people may equally present frustrations in ugly ways.

This could affect my learning if something negatively impacts me or a project and I don’t mention it, when it’s perfectly reasonable to do so. I strive to be a clear communicator and try to remember my belief that “everyone is good inside”, when confrontations arise.

Share an example of when you were trying to work productively with others, but there was resistance or tension. Discuss strategies you tried at the time, how effective they were, and your reflections on what other strategies you would try now and why.

Working in the Haystack cafe in Wales as a chef - We would often have busy breakfast/lunch rushes. I would get my head down and push through it, possibly resenting how hard I had to work but not voicing it. From my perspective the kitchen felt way under the pump and I was normally in there solo. I don’t think I did much about it at the time apart from just working really hard, trying to clear all the dockets & possibly grunting with frustration.

Now I think I would approach it by communicating more frequently with the managers about being overwhelmed and needing help, plus in the moment asking if they can send someone in to help get orders out. If you don’t voice your struggles then no one can help you. I would also come up with a system strategy i.e. that helper can plate up while I cook. Sometimes the reason getting help was difficult was because it was an extra mental load to figure out what they should do. Getting some photos of the dishes to put up on the wall would also help to make sure that they knew what to do. So I would be more proactive in all those areas.
