Neuroplasticity

How does understanding the principles of neuroplasticity benefit people?

There have been studies that have shown that when people understand how their brain works, that it is malleable, and can develop/strengthen neural pathways, they often achieve greater learning outcomes than control groups who are not taught about neuroplasticity.

Simply put, if you know that your brain can develop new pathways and learn new things, then you can put that into practice. If you don’t know that, and have a belief that your brain is fixed or that “you're not smart”, then you are unlikely to persist at trying to understand and learn new things, because you just don’t think it’s possible.

How will I engage with the principles of neuroplasticity for my benefit?

Daily practice of all of the things I want to get better at: coding, relationships, communication, German, problem solving, resisting distracting unproductive temptations, breathing well, mindfulness, gratefulness, physical strength/flexibility/fitness.

By engaging in skills daily you stimulate those neural pathways, and they become stronger and stronger because they grow just as a muscle would if you continuously used it.

Stick with things that are challenging, and continue to go back to them, with the understanding that practicing will increase the “strength” of my brain to do those things. If you give up because something is too challenging, your brain will just give up on trying to do that thing too, but if you can stick with it and keep trying, try out some different angles, try get some hints to help you overcome the block, then your brain is learning to engage those neural pathways.

Some of the ways to increase your neuroplasticity

  • Practice practice practice - the more you do something, the more likely the neurons associated with that thing are likely to fire.
  • Practice skills in your imagination - studies have shown that using your imagination to practice a skill when you don’t have the physical ability to do it at that moment can improve that skill. Just don’t forget to physically practice too!
  • Put information into context - a great way to do this is by integrating learning subjects with projects relevant to your personal life. Learn how different concepts and information relate to each other. Understanding how the pieces fit together helps you to understand the individual pieces.
  • Do challenging things outside of your comfort zone - to keep learning new things, and push the limits of what you are capable of you have to get a little bit outside your comfort zone. Eventually your neural pathways will become so familiar with that “uncomfortable” thing that it will be comfortable.
  • Physical exercise - improves how efficiently the brain uses glucose for energy, and may also increase hippocampus size, while practicing coordination of many moving parts.
  • Eat well, our brain uses a quarter of our food intake. Blueberries, walnuts, avocado, magnesium, Vitamin D are all great choices. As well as eating a diet with less processed foods.
  • 20 min afternoon naps can apparently help, however a great alternative to a 20 min nap is 20 mins of NSDR or yoga nidra

Growth Mindset

What is it and why is it relevant?

Growth mindset is the understanding that your brain is malleable and that you can learn anything, while understanding that even if it is truly difficult and foreign to you, it’s just because you don’t understand it well enough yet, but if you continue trying you can & will get there.

Whereas a fixed mindset is where you believe that other people are just talented, and you are not, you will never be able to do those things.

This is relevant because having a fixed mindset is very limiting. It’s blatantly not true, but causes people to get stuck believing they can’t learn, achieve more, or grow. Instead they fully believe that they are just not capable. However, once you understand and adapt a growth mindset, those beliefs are blown away, because you understand that anything is possible, as long as you apply yourself, break it down into smaller parts, and/or learn from others.

No one is born being able to paint a masterpiece, build things, or having a vast understanding of how the world works, it’s all learnt, and we can learn it too.

What surprised and changed for me during this exploration

I now feel much more empowered about learning new things.

That the neurons, synapses & connections that we are born with are not finite, that despite things that we might have done to harm our brains or dull growth, we can actually always do more to develop & rewire our brains.

I enjoyed the idea that “confidence is just displayed ability”. So if you want to build confidence, proving that you can do something by having some small wins, or looking back at the things that you have achieved can help you to believe that you are capable, and by continuously proving that to yourself, your belief in yourself becomes stronger and stronger.

I also noticed how some people in my life can tend towards a fixed mindset, and I would love to pass this knowledge on to them to help them understand that they can learn anything too! I have told them that in the past but not with science.

How I will integrate a growth mindset into my learning journey

If something ever feels impossible, I will take a moment to remember that I just don’t get it yet, but I can learn it.

I will remember that if someone else can do something, so can I.

I will celebrate the little wins
